The Whisperings of Annexation

With Kalākaua advocating for a more independent Hawaiʻi and , there was a rumbling among the white population of Hawaiʻi. The descendants of the first missionaries that arrived in Hawaiʻi had enjoyed succesful careers as plantation owners, government officials and merchants. However, for years there were talks of annexation among both the white population of Hawaiʻi and United States government. The call to annexation were because of five main reasons: the decline of the Native population, Hawaiʻi's economic and military significance, fear of other countries taking Hawaiʻi for themselves and manifest destiny

Low Population of Hawaiians

By 1866, There was around 60,000 Native Hawaiians left. Just sixteen years earlier, there was 85,000. Americans believed that the Hawaiian race would die out within one or two generations. With so few Hawaiian then, why should they continue to rule? U.S Minister James McBryde stated "that within a short period, say from 20-40years, there will not be enough of them remaining to perpetuate this government. This being the case, these islands must, out of necessity, pass into other hands and their destiny controlled by other people."(Budnick, 32)

Military and Economic Significance

Hawaiʻi was in a perfect position in the Pacific to place a naval base. The United States really wanted to obtain Pearl Harbour, which would give them a foothold in the Pacific both militarily and economically. Major General Schofield and Brigadier General B.S Alexander spent two months as a "vacation" in Honolulu in 1873 to inspect the legitmacy of Pearl Harbour as a naval base. They found that Pearl Harbour would be an extremely valuable asset to obtain, and that Hawaiʻi should be taken by either a treaty or annexation. While the U.S did receive Pearl Harbor from Kalākaua's Reciprocity Treaty, it was on loan for only 7 years. America wanted it permanantly

Fear of Other Countires

The U.S feared that this valuable asset would be used against them by other powerful nations. Great Britain, France and Germany had already colonized a majority of the Pacific, while America was busy taking over the northern continent. They feared in falling behind, and annexing Hawaiʻi as a territory could give them a start in increasing the size and influence of their empire.

Manifest Destiny

Manifest Destiny was a belief that it was America's God-given destiny to spread out across the continent and become a great nation. This was involved in the Mexican-American war, and soon became involved in the idea of annexing Hawaiʻi. To these men of the 19th century, it was simply America's destiny to colonize and control new nations that would provide new opportunities for their great nation.

The Hawaiian League take action

In reaction to Kalākaua's attempt to establish a Polynesian empire and his lavish spending habits, the secret Hawaiian League was created. The goal of this all-white League was to create a constituational republic made up of themselves. They felt the king's power was absolute, and that he needed to be dethroned. However, they did not want to establish a new constitution for the betterment of the citizens of Hawaiʻi, but to give Caucasians more rights and restrict Native Hawaiian's political rights.

The Hawaiian League soon allied themselves with an all-Caucasian para-military organization called the Honolulu Rifles. By July 6, Hawaiian League founder Lorrin Thurston had wrote up a new constitution, which would be forced upon Kalākaua with the threat of the Honolulu Rifles.

The Bayonet Constitution of 1887

On the morning of July 6th, Honolulu Rifle leader Colonel Volney Ashford attacked and seized a shipment of arms and marched with a squad of Honolulu Rifles to Hawaiʻi Prime Minister Walter M. Gibson's home, arresting and threatening to kill him. Kalākaua soon caught wind of the Honolulu Rifles, who then approached him with Thurston's constitution. Kalākaua was then forced to sign the constitution after a few hours of arguing. A new Cabinet was soon appointed, that being made up of five Hawaiian League members. Because of the threats of violence the king was under, the Constitution of 1887 soon became known as the Bayonet Constitution

The Bayonet Constitution included major changes to Hawaiʻi government, the largest of which was greatly restricting the monarch's power. The King could no longer act without approval of the Cabinet, and the Cabinet could only be changed by the Legislature. Another major shift was in that of voting regulations. Property and income requirements increased significantly, and all Asia-born citizens were excluded from voting. The new requirements shifted voting power towards the white men of Hawaiʻi, since most Native Hawaiians did not have the amount of income or land the new Constitution deemed necessary. It also completely excluded almost all of the immigrants from voting, except for the Portugese, who allowed to vote under the Constitution's rules.

King Kalākaua was virtually disposed as a monarch, holding almost zero power in the Hawaiian government. The revolution was a complete success, and foreigner's now controlled Hawaiʻi's government. The new Constitution was extremely disliked by the Native Hawaiians, who would send resolutions asking for a return to the previous constitution. In 1899, two years after the Bayonet Constitution, an insurrection was attempted. Led by the aliʻi Robert Wilcox, a hundred insurrectionists occupied the ʻIolani Palace's grounds. It was soon crushed by American soldiers, who were able to help stop a violent insurrection by Hawaiians, but not from white people. The writing on the wall was clear: Hawaiʻi's government was no longer in the hands of Hawaiians.

Kalākaua died on January 30th, 1891. He was a king that always pushed for the advancement of Hawaiʻi and it's people. While seen as extravagant and even cowardly at the time, Kalākaua is now appreciated today for his commitment to restoring Hawaiian culture.
